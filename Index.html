Boat & Dock Hinges: Hardware for Constant Salt Exposure

On the water there's no dry season. Grade, fastener, and finish all have to assume permanent chloride contact.

Updated Jun 2026·7 min read·Reviewed by a licensed PE

Marine installs punish every shortcut. The hinge that lasts a decade on a garden gate can streak rust within months on a dock box, because salt air keeps a thin chloride film on the metal around the clock. The fix isn't exotic — it's discipline about three things.

316 only — no exceptions near salt

The molybdenum in 316 is what resists chloride pitting. On a boat or dock, 304 will tea-stain and eventually pit; specify 316 (marine grade, A4) for every leaf and pin.

Field checklist
316 leaf + 316 pin · 316 fasteners (never zinc or carbon) · rinse with fresh water periodically · re-passivate if you cut or drill the hinge on site.

Match the fastener or lose the install

A 316 hinge with carbon-steel screws sets up a galvanic cell — the screws corrode first and the joint loosens. Keep the whole assembly in the same stainless family, and bed fasteners in a marine sealant to keep water out of the threads.

Salt doesn't care what the hinge is rated to hold. It attacks the weakest metal in the joint — usually the fastener nobody specified.

The takeaway

Corrosion resistance is a system property. Get the grade, the fastener, and the finish to agree, and a marine hinge will outlast the hardware around it. Compromise on any one and the salt finds it.
