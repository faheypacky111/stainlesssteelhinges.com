Buying guide

The best gate hinges

Reviewed June 2026 · independent, spec-first guidance

Gates live outdoors and swing hard. The right hinge depends on the gate's material, weight, and whether it needs to self-close.

What to look for

01

Match the gate material

Wood gates take strap or T-hinges; metal gates take weld-on or bolt-on barrel hinges.

02

Size to the weight

Heavier gates need longer straps or barrel hinges and often three hinges instead of two.

03

Grade for exposure

316 near the coast; 304 or hot-dip galvanized inland. Pool gates should be 316 and self-closing.

04

Self-closing where required

Pool and safety gates need self-closing, self-latching hardware by code.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Under-hinging a heavy gate so it sags and drags within a season.
  • Using interior-grade or plated hinges outdoors — 304 inland, 316 near salt.
  • Screwing strap hinges into end grain or thin pickets; hit the frame or rail instead.
  • Forgetting self-closing and self-latching where pool codes require it.
  • Mixing carbon-steel bolts with stainless hinges (rust streaks and galvanic corrosion).

Frequently asked questions

How do I stop a gate from sagging?

Use enough hinges for the weight, add an anti-sag turnbuckle cable corner to corner, and consider adjustable hinges. Fasten hardware to the frame, not the pickets.

Weld-on or bolt-on gate hinges?

Weld-on are strongest for metal gates; bolt- or lag-on suit wood and let you adjust later. Match the grade to your climate either way.

How many hinges does a gate need?

Light gates: two. Tall or heavy gates: three, to stop sag and twist.

Do pool gates have special rules?

Yes — most codes require self-closing and self-latching hardware with the latch at a set height. Check your local code.
