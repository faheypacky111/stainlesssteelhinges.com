Buying guide

The best marine-grade hinges

Marine duty means salt, constantly. That narrows the field to 316 and a few specifics that separate hardware that lasts from hardware that pits.

What to look for

01

316, confirmed

Accept nothing less than 316 (A4). "Marine" or "rust resistant" without a grade is a red flag near salt.

02

Matching 316 fasteners

Mixing grades or metals invites galvanic corrosion. Use 316 screws with 316 hinges.

03

Sealed or weld-on as needed

For decks and hatches, choose the mounting style that suits the substrate; cast 316 holds up well in splash zones.

04

Finish for tea-stain resistance

An electropolished or well-finished surface resists coastal tea staining better.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using 304 near salt to save money — it will pit.
  • Mixing 316 hinges with carbon-steel or 304 fasteners (galvanic corrosion).
  • Trapping salt in crevices without rinsing (crevice corrosion even on 316).
  • Assuming shiny means marine; verify the 316 / A4 stamp.
  • Skipping bedding compound on through-bolts, inviting crevice corrosion.

Frequently asked questions

Is 316 fully rust-proof?

No metal is. 316 strongly resists chloride pitting, but harsh salt can still surface-stain it. Rinse with fresh water and it cleans up — it won't structurally pit like 304.

Do I need 316 screws too?

Yes. Pairing 316 hinges with lesser fasteners creates a galvanic cell and the screws corrode first. Match the fastener grade to the hinge.

Is 316 enough for direct saltwater immersion?

For most above-water marine hardware, yes. For permanent immersion or aggressive splash zones, duplex grades resist even better — but that's rarely needed for hinges.

Do I really need 316 screws too?

Yes. Carbon-steel or even 304 screws in a 316 hinge can corrode and stain. Match the grade.
