Buying guide

The best commercial door hinges

Commercial doors cycle thousands of times and often carry code requirements. That pushes you toward ball-bearing template hinges or continuous geared hinges.

What to look for

01

Template hinges

Commercial hinges follow standard template hole patterns to fit hollow-metal frames — match the template.

02

Cycle rating

High-traffic openings need heavy-weight ball-bearing hinges rated for millions of cycles.

03

Continuous geared option

Full-height continuous geared hinges spread stress and resist sag on abused doors — common on schools and hospitals.

04

Code and fire ratings

Confirm fire-door and accessibility requirements for the opening before specifying.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Speccing a plain-bearing hinge on a high-cycle door — it drags within a year.
  • Ignoring ANSI/BHMA grade; Grade 3 residential hinges fail fast in commercial traffic.
  • Using non-NRP hinges on out-swing exterior doors (a security hole).
  • Putting a non-fire-rated hinge on a fire door, which voids the label.
  • Under-sizing hinge count or width for heavy solid-core doors.

Frequently asked questions

What ANSI grade do I need?

Grade 1 (BHMA A156.1) for high-traffic and exterior commercial doors; Grade 2 for moderate use. Grade 3 is residential and won't hold up in a busy building.

When should I use a continuous hinge?

On heavy, high-abuse, or sag-prone doors. A geared continuous hinge carries load along the full height, eliminating the point stress that makes butt hinges sag.

What is a template hinge?

A hinge drilled to a standard hole pattern (template) that matches commercial hollow-metal frames so it bolts straight in.

Continuous or butt hinges for commercial?

Butt (template) hinges suit most openings; continuous geared hinges are chosen for high-abuse, high-traffic doors where sag and pull-off are concerns.
